Contentment Creates Happiness

DEARLY BELOVED IN CHRIST,

Socrates once said, “Contentment is natural wealth; luxury is artificial poverty”. Solomon also prayed as written in Prov: 30:7-9.  The Epistle of St. Paul to Philippians (Phi: 4:12-13) describes Paul’s response to life.  Paul’s relationship with God superseded whatever he did or did not have.  His contentment was not based on his circumstances, but on his relationship with Christ.  St. Paul reminds us that contentment doesn’t happen overnight.  It’s something that we learn.  As our relationship with God develops over time and through experiences, we learn to trust God more and ourselves less.  The starting point is truly trusting God, and allowing God to show us the way.  We have to make the right choices in life if we have to be contented.  Contentment is the key to happiness.  Contentment is the aptitude to articulate, “Things and money cannot buy me happiness, but God and loving relationships can.”   The more we learn to respect and love people, and not materials; the more we learn to use materials, and not live for them - we learn the art of contentment.  Contentedness is a matter of being satisfied with what we have.  We should stop, looking at other people’s possession with jealousy and have cravings for those, because it will never make us happy.  Currently, the world is in financial turmoil because of virtual money, and lack of contentment.  We are not satisfied with what we have, thus we are depleting the earth its natural resources.  If one is not satisfied or contented, no amount of money can attain what we are searching for.  There is no end to senseless craving.  Once you have learned to be content, you don’t need more.  You may stop acquiring, and start enjoying.  People are working so hard to earn money, and towards the end they do not know the purpose behind such a stressful life.

Contentment is not just related to money and things but also to people and other essentials like a job.  Happiness is the fruit of accepting whatever God has given us – our job, our families, our children etc.  Happiness is an inside job and being content is essential for happiness.  One day, the father of a very wealthy family took his son on a trip to the country with the single purpose of showing him how poor people live.  They spent couple of days and nights on the farm, what would be considered a very poor family.  On their return from their trip the father asked his son. “How was the trip?” Son replied, “It was great, Dad.”  “Did you see how poor people live?” father asked.  “Oh yeah, said the son.  “So, tell me, what you learned from the trip?” asked the father.  The son answered: “I saw that we have one dog and they had four.  We have a pool that reaches to the middle of our garden, and they have a creek that has no end.  We have imported lanterns in our garden, and they have the stars at night.  Our patio reaches to the front yard and they have the whole horizon.  We have a small piece of land to live on and they have fields that go beyond our sight.  We have servants who serve us, but they serve others.  We buy our food, but they grow theirs. We have walls around our property to protect us; they have friends to protect them.  The boy’s father was speechless.  Then his son added.  “Thanks Dad for showing me how poor we are.”  Isn’t perspective a wonderful thing? Makes you wonder what would happen if we all gave thanks for everything we have, instead of worrying about what we don’t have.  So, it is not what we have that really matters but our perspective.  Contentment is a matter of the heart.  A key word for attitude towards happiness and wellbeing is contentment.  May the Good Lord Bless Us All.

                                                                                 Yours in His Service,

Sujan Achen.
